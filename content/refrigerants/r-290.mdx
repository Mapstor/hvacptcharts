narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-290 is pure propane (C₃H₈) — a single-component hydrocarbon refrigerant, not a blend. It has been used in industrial refrigeration since the 19th century and is the dominant household-refrigerator refrigerant globally outside the US.

    R-290 is ASHRAE class A3: lower toxicity, highly flammable with a burning velocity above 100 cm/s. This is materially different from any HFC or HFO refrigerant. Equipment design, charge limits, ventilation, leak detection, and service procedures must all account for the flammability hazard.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Household refrigerators and freezers (dominant globally; expanding in the US under SNAP rule updates)
    - Wine coolers, beverage merchandisers, small commercial display cases
    - Plug-in commercial refrigeration (charge-limited applications)
    - Heat pumps (small residential, R-290 monobloc designs growing)
    - Industrial process cooling

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-290 itself is not phase-down-targeted — its GWP of 3 is below every regulatory threshold. The constraint is flammability. EPA SNAP rules and UL 60335-2-89 charge limits restrict the maximum refrigerant charge per circuit based on application:

    - Household refrigerators: 150 grams (~5.3 oz)
    - Small commercial refrigeration: 500 grams (recently raised from 150 g for certain self-contained applications)
    - Larger commercial / industrial: subject to ventilation and machine room requirements

  serviceNotes: |
    Mineral oil (MO) is the lubricant standard for propane systems. POE is NOT used.

    Service requires HC-rated equipment design, sealed systems where practical, and adherence to charge limits. Refrigerant must be recovered with HC-rated recovery equipment; never vent.

    Brazing and any open flame must occur AFTER the system has been thoroughly evacuated of refrigerant. A leak detector specifically calibrated for hydrocarbons (not halogenated refrigerants) is required for leak detection.

faqs:
  - q: "Is R-290 the same as the propane I use for grilling?"
    a: |
      Chemically yes, but refrigerant-grade R-290 is purified to a much higher standard. Refrigerant propane is specified to ≥99.5% purity with strict limits on moisture, sulfur, and other contaminants. Commercial-grade propane (like LPG for grills) contains odorants, ethyl mercaptan, and other impurities that would damage refrigeration system seals and components.

  - q: "What lubricant does R-290 use?"
    a: |
      Mineral oil (MO) is the standard. Alkylbenzene (AB) and polyalphaolefin (PAO) are also compatible. POE is NOT used with R-290 in original systems.

  - q: "Is R-290 dangerous to work with?"
    a: |
      It is highly flammable (ASHRAE A3) and requires appropriate procedures. The risks are manageable in well-designed systems with proper charge limits, ventilation, and service procedures. Most R-290 equipment is factory-sealed; field service is limited to leak repair and recovery rather than open-system work. EPA Section 608 certification plus equipment-specific HC training are typical service prerequisites.

  - q: "What is the GWP of R-290?"
    a: |
      Approximately 3 (IPCC AR5) — effectively zero compared with HFC refrigerants. R-290 atmospheric lifetime is only about two weeks. This makes it one of the lowest-impact refrigerant choices available, contingent on the flammability constraint being manageable for the application.

  - q: "What is the normal operating pressure of R-290?"
    a: |
      Similar to R-22. At 95°F outdoor ambient, expect roughly 65 PSIG suction and 220 PSIG discharge on a small R-290 system. Saturation pressure at 95°F is 175 PSIG. Standard R-22-rated gauges, hoses, and recovery equipment work with R-290 from a pressure standpoint, but must be confirmed HC-compatible (most halogenated-refrigerant equipment uses elastomers attacked by hydrocarbons).

retrofitGuidance:
  fromRefrigerant: r-22
  notes: |
    Drop-in retrofit is generally not recommended due to flammability. The original system must be HC-rated for safety: brushless electrical components, sealed electrics in the refrigerant circuit, no open flames in the operating envelope, and an explosion-safe enclosure for service. Most retrofits are equipment replacement with new HC-rated equipment, not refrigerant swap.
