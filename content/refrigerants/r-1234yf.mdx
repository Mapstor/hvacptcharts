narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-1234yf is pure 2,3,3,3-tetrafluoropropene (CF₃CF=CH₂), a single-molecule hydrofluoroolefin (HFO). The double bond in the molecule's structure makes it reactive with atmospheric hydroxyl radicals, breaking down in roughly two weeks rather than persisting for years like the saturated HFCs.

    R-1234yf was developed jointly by Honeywell and DuPont/Chemours in the 2000s as the engineered low-GWP replacement for R-134a in mobile air conditioning. The blend matches R-134a's saturation pressure envelope closely (within a few PSI across the operating range), allowing vehicle OEMs to convert their AC system designs with relatively modest changes — primarily the addition of A2L-compatible safety features.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Mobile air conditioning (passenger vehicles globally since ~2014; the dominant choice for most OEMs)
    - Light commercial refrigeration (component in blends and standalone in some applications)
    - Component in R-448A, R-449A, R-452A, R-452B, R-454B, R-454C, R-455A, R-457A, R-513A, R-516A blends
    - Some industrial chiller applications

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-1234yf itself is not phase-down-targeted — its GWP of 4 (IPCC AR5) or roughly 0.5 (IPCC AR6) is below every regulatory threshold. The constraint is the A2L safety class and the cost of production relative to R-134a; commercial pricing per pound has historically been ~5–10× R-134a's pre-phase-down prices.

    For mobile AC the transition from R-134a to R-1234yf is essentially complete in major markets — most passenger vehicles produced since approximately 2017 in the US and EU use R-1234yf. The IRS has phased the R-134a service supply down via AIM Act baselines, which has further accelerated the mobile-AC industry's standardization on R-1234yf.

  serviceNotes: |
    Mobile AC uses Polyalkylene Glycol (PAG) oil — the same lubricant family as R-134a mobile AC systems. Stationary applications use Polyolester (POE). The two are NOT interchangeable; do not cross-contaminate. PAG-and-POE specifications for R-1234yf systems are similar to R-134a but typically with tighter water content limits.

    A2L safety class requires: no open flames in the service area; A2L-rated recovery cylinders (yellow with red top stripe); leak detection equipment specifically rated for A2L hydrofluoroolefins; technician training on A2L handling. Mobile AC service in particular requires SAE J2843-compliant recovery and recharge equipment with refrigerant identification capability — R-1234yf and R-134a must not be cross-charged.

faqs:
  - q: "What is the normal operating pressure of R-1234yf?"
    a: "Very similar to R-134a — within a few PSI across the operating range. R-1234yf saturation at 70°F is ~74 PSIG; R-134a is ~71 PSIG. Mobile AC operating pressures vary substantially with compressor speed and condenser airflow but typically: suction 25–45 PSIG, discharge 150–300 PSIG depending on ambient and engine RPM. Stationary chiller and commercial refrigeration applications operate in narrower windows."

  - q: "Why does R-1234yf cost so much more than R-134a?"
    a: "Production complexity and patent licensing. R-1234yf's synthesis requires multiple specialized steps and proprietary catalysts (Honeywell and Chemours hold many of the relevant patents). The molecule is also less stable than R-134a, requiring careful handling and quality control in manufacturing. As production scaled and patents began expiring in the late 2020s, the price premium has been narrowing — but per-pound pricing remains meaningfully higher than legacy HFCs."

  - q: "How does R-1234yf compare to R-134a?"
    a: "Saturation pressures very close (R-1234yf 74 vs R-134a 71 PSIG at 70°F). Both are non-toxic (Class A). The substantive differences: GWP (R-1234yf 4 vs R-134a 1430), atmospheric lifetime (R-1234yf ~12 days vs R-134a ~14 years), and safety class (R-1234yf A2L mildly flammable vs R-134a A1 non-flammable). For mobile AC the A2L class required revised vehicle safety standards but the lower GWP made it the regulatory path forward."

  - q: "Is R-1234yf safe for cars?"
    a: "Yes when handled per A2L procedures. The mild flammability (low burning velocity, narrow flammability range in air) is engineered around through hardware: hermetic system design, no open electrical connections in the refrigerant circuit, specific evacuation and recharge procedures. Crash safety has been studied extensively — including the controversial Daimler test results from the early 2010s — and current OEMs have addressed the engineering concerns with system design changes. EPA and global regulators have endorsed R-1234yf for mobile AC."

  - q: "What lubricant does R-1234yf use?"
    a: "Mobile AC: Polyalkylene Glycol (PAG) oil. Stationary applications: Polyolester (POE). The two are not interchangeable. For mobile AC retrofits from R-134a, the PAG specifications differ slightly (R-1234yf systems typically use 'PAG-Y' or PAG-double-end-capped formulations) but most R-134a PAG oils are functionally compatible. Always reference the OEM service manual for the specific system."

  - q: "Can I retrofit an R-134a vehicle to R-1234yf?"
    a: "Technically possible but not generally recommended. The pressure differences are small enough that the system would operate, but R-134a vehicles lack the A2L safety features (specific recovery equipment compatibility, leak detection, hermetic seal upgrades) that R-1234yf systems are designed around. Most major OEMs do not endorse R-134a-to-R-1234yf retrofits in vehicles originally built for R-134a; the realistic service path for legacy R-134a vehicles is continued R-134a service (with reclaimed material as supply tightens)."

  - q: "What's the atmospheric lifetime of R-1234yf?"
    a: "Approximately 12 days. The molecule's carbon-carbon double bond reacts rapidly with atmospheric hydroxyl radicals, breaking it down to trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) which deposits via rainfall. The TFA fate has been studied for environmental and water-system impacts; current research suggests environmental concentrations remain well below thresholds of concern even at full deployment. Short atmospheric lifetime is the structural reason for R-1234yf's very low GWP."

  fromRefrigerant: r-134a

    Vehicle retrofits from R-134a to R-1234yf are technically possible (similar pressures, compatible PAG-family lubricants) but not generally recommended by OEMs. The realistic application is using R-1234yf in vehicles originally designed and certified for R-1234yf; legacy R-134a vehicles continue to use R-134a until end of service life. Stationary chiller and commercial refrigeration retrofits from R-134a to R-1234yf require A2L equipment certification (compressor, safety systems, electrical) — generally a full equipment replacement rather than a refrigerant swap.
