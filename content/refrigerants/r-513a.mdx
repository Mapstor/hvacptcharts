narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-513A is a binary near-azeotropic blend of 44% R-134a and 56% R-1234yf by mass. The composition was engineered as the lowest-GWP non-flammable replacement for R-134a in centrifugal chillers and medium-temperature commercial refrigeration — applications where A2L safety class is operationally inconvenient and the GWP reduction matters.

    Like R-450A (a related Opteon-line blend), R-513A keeps the A1 safety class by using R-134a as the non-flammable backbone, with R-1234yf substituted to drive down the overall GWP. The result is GWP 631 — well below R-134a's 1430 and within the AIM Act 700 threshold for new equipment.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Centrifugal chillers (medium-pressure commercial cooling)
    - Screw chillers
    - Medium-temperature commercial refrigeration (supermarket cases)
    - Heat pumps (some commercial applications)
    - R-134a retrofit (direct, A1-to-A1)

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-513A's GWP of 631 is below the EPA AIM Act 700 threshold for new equipment in most chiller and commercial refrigeration categories — it remains compliant for new installations under current US regulations. It does not satisfy the EU F-Gas 150 threshold; EU long-term paths use R-1234ze(E) (A2L, GWP 7), R-515A/B (A1, GWP ~290-390), or R-1234yf-dominant A2L blends.

    For the US chiller market R-513A is positioned alongside R-450A as the primary "stay-A1" R-134a alternative. Equipment OEMs (Carrier, Trane, York, Daikin) publish equipment-specific compatibility lists; for most R-134a chiller designs R-513A is an OEM-approved fill option.

  serviceNotes: |
    Polyolester (POE) oil is required. R-513A is near-azeotropic — temperature glide approximately 0°F (less than 0.5°F at any operating point). For all practical purposes the blend behaves as a pure refrigerant in service.

    R-513A pressures are within ~5% of R-134a's across the operating range. For R-134a retrofits the expansion valve typically requires no adjustment and the system pressure-bearing components are pressure-rated for R-513A without modification.

faqs:
  - q: "What is R-513A also called?"
    a: "R-513A is the ASHRAE 34 designation. Primary commercial brands are Honeywell Solstice N13 and Chemours Opteon XP10. (The Opteon XP10 brand is also used by Chemours for R-450A in some product lines — verify the specific Opteon SKU on the cylinder marking, since Opteon XP10 has historically been used for both R-450A and R-513A in different product references.)"

  - q: "How does R-513A compare to R-134a?"
    a: "Pressures within ~5% — at 70°F R-513A is 77 PSIG vs R-134a's 71 PSIG. Both are A1 non-flammable; both use POE oil; both are near-azeotropic. The substantive differences are GWP (R-513A 631 vs R-134a 1430) and the blend composition (R-513A includes R-1234yf which drives down the GWP). For most R-134a applications R-513A is a direct drop-in once the equipment OEM has certified compatibility."

  - q: "R-513A or R-450A for an R-134a retrofit?"
    a: "Both are A1 binary blends with R-134a as a component; both are near-azeotropic; both target the same applications. R-513A is 44/56 R-134a/R-1234yf with GWP 631; R-450A is 42/58 R-134a/R-1234ze(E) with GWP 605. Performance is very similar. The choice depends on equipment manufacturer compatibility lists (some OEMs approve only one), distributor availability, and operating preference. Both follow the same retrofit procedure."

  - q: "What lubricant does R-513A use?"
    a: "Polyolester (POE) oil. Mineral oil and alkylbenzene are not miscible. POE is hygroscopic; deep vacuum and verified hold matter before charging."

  - q: "Can I drop R-513A into an R-134a chiller?"
    a: "In many cases yes, with relatively modest service work. Both use POE oil; pressures are within 5%; the expansion valve typically doesn't need adjustment for a near-azeotrope. The key requirement is the equipment OEM's R-513A compatibility certification — verify before retrofitting. Procedure: recover R-134a, verify POE in the system, replace filter-drier with POE-compatible model, pull deep vacuum, charge R-513A by weight per the system's calculated capacity."

  - q: "Why is R-134a being replaced?"
    a: "GWP. R-134a's 1430 places it above the EPA AIM Act 700 threshold for new equipment in most categories. For chillers and commercial refrigeration, R-513A and R-450A are the primary A1 (non-flammable) replacements; R-1234yf, R-1234ze(E), and R-516A are A2L alternatives with even lower GWP."

  - q: "What is the normal operating pressure of R-513A?"
    a: "Very close to R-134a. On a typical commercial chiller with 45°F leaving water temperature and 95°F outdoor ambient, expect roughly 35-50 PSIG suction and 130-170 PSIG discharge. Saturation at 70°F is 77 PSIG; at 100°F it's ~140 PSIG. Operating pressures depend on chiller load, condenser approach, and evaporator setpoint."

retrofitGuidance:
  fromRefrigerant: r-134a
  notes: |
    R-134a to R-513A is among the simpler HFC retrofits: both A1 (no safety class change), both POE (no oil change), pressures within 5% (no expansion valve adjustment typically required for near-azeotropic). Procedure: recover R-134a, verify POE in the system, replace filter-drier with POE-compatible model, pull vacuum to 500 microns held 30 minutes, charge R-513A by weight per the system's calculated capacity. The key gate is equipment OEM compatibility certification — verify R-513A is on the approved refrigerant list for the specific equipment before retrofitting.
