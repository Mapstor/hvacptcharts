narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-1234ze is pure 1,3,3,3-tetrafluoropropene (the E-isomer, CHF=CHCF₃). It's a single-molecule HFO developed jointly by Honeywell and chemistry partners as a very-low-GWP refrigerant for chillers and commercial refrigeration applications where R-134a's GWP is restricted.

    Saturation pressures are substantially lower than R-134a — about 30% lower — which reduces volumetric capacity per unit displacement. R-1234ze chillers are physically larger than R-134a equivalents at the same cooling capacity. The lower pressure makes it a natural fit for low-pressure centrifugal chiller designs.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Centrifugal chillers (low-pressure commercial cooling, large office buildings)
    - Heat pumps (some commercial applications, particularly EU market)
    - Foam blowing agent (polyurethane insulation)
    - Aerosol propellant
    - Component in R-450A, R-515A, R-515B blends

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-1234ze is not phase-down-targeted — GWP 7 (IPCC AR5) or about 1.4 (IPCC AR6) is below every regulatory threshold. Constraints are the A2L safety class (requires A2L-rated equipment and procedures), the lower volumetric capacity vs R-134a (larger equipment), and the cost premium per pound of refrigerant.

    For new low-pressure centrifugal chiller installations in regions with strong climate regulation (EU, increasingly US), R-1234ze is positioned as the long-term replacement for R-134a equivalent capacity classes. The R-134a-to-R-1234ze transition requires equipment redesign for A2L safety; existing R-134a chillers continue in service with reclaimed R-134a or transition to lower-GWP A1 alternatives (R-450A, R-513A, R-515B) instead.

  serviceNotes: |
    Polyolester (POE) oil is required for refrigeration applications. Some specific chiller designs use Polyalkylene Glycol (PAG) — verify per the equipment OEM. Mineral oil is not used.

    A2L safety class requires: A2L-rated equipment design, leak detection, no open flames during service, A2L-rated recovery cylinders (yellow with red top stripe). For machine-room installations specific ventilation requirements per ASHRAE 15 apply at the larger charge sizes typical of commercial chillers.

faqs:
  - q: "What is R-1234ze also called?"
    a: "R-1234ze is the ASHRAE 34 designation for the E-isomer (the more common form). Its primary commercial brand is Honeywell Solstice ze. The Z-isomer (R-1234ze(Z)) is a separate, less-common refrigerant used in high-temperature heat pumps and organic Rankine cycle applications. When 'R-1234ze' is referenced without qualifier, the E-isomer is meant by convention."

  - q: "How does R-1234ze compare to R-134a?"
    a: "Saturation pressures are about 30% lower — R-1234ze at 70°F is ~49 PSIG vs R-134a's 71 PSIG. Volumetric capacity is correspondingly lower; an R-1234ze chiller is physically larger than an R-134a equivalent at the same cooling capacity. GWP: R-1234ze 7 vs R-134a 1430 (a 99.5% reduction). Safety class: R-1234ze A2L mildly flammable vs R-134a A1 non-flammable."

  - q: "Why is R-1234ze used in chillers instead of R-1234yf?"
    a: "Pressure envelope match. R-1234ze's lower saturation pressure better matches the design envelope of low-pressure centrifugal chillers, where pressures are typically below atmospheric on the evaporator side. R-1234yf (used dominantly in mobile AC) has higher pressures more suited to compact compressor designs. Each HFO has its application niche based on the operating pressure envelope it best matches."

  - q: "What lubricant does R-1234ze use?"
    a: "Polyolester (POE) oil for most applications. Some specific chiller OEMs use Polyalkylene Glycol (PAG) — verify per equipment service literature. Mineral oil is not used with R-1234ze."

  - q: "Can I retrofit an R-134a chiller to R-1234ze?"
    a: "Not as a drop-in. The substantially lower pressure envelope of R-1234ze means an R-134a-designed chiller will lose ~25-30% capacity if refrigerant-swapped — the compressor isn't sized for the lower mass flow per unit volume. The A2L safety class adds equipment-level requirements (leak detection, ventilation). In practice the transition is full equipment replacement with new R-1234ze-rated chillers, not refrigerant retrofit."

  - q: "Is R-1234ze flammable?"
    a: "Yes — ASHRAE class A2L, mildly flammable with low burning velocity. The flammability characteristics are similar to R-32 and other HFO blends: limited flame propagation in air, manageable through A2L-rated equipment design and service procedures. Not a substantial hazard in properly-engineered installations but materially different from A1 non-flammable refrigerants."

    Refrigerant-only retrofit is not feasible due to the substantial pressure envelope difference (~30% lower) and capacity mismatch. The realistic transition for R-134a chillers facing GWP restrictions is full equipment replacement with R-1234ze-rated chillers (A2L), R-1234ze-blend chillers (R-450A, R-515B — A1), or with other A2L refrigerants in their pressure-appropriate equipment.
