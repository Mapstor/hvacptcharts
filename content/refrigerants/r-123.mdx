narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-123 is pure 2,2-dichloro-1,1,1-trifluoroethane — a hydrochlorofluorocarbon developed as the long-term replacement for R-11 in low-pressure centrifugal chillers when CFCs were phased out under the Montreal Protocol in the 1990s. R-123's modest ozone-depletion potential (ODP 0.02) and very low GWP (77) made it the standard for large building cooling for two decades.

    R-123 is unique among common HVAC refrigerants in being classified ASHRAE B1 — higher toxicity (Occupational Exposure Limit below 400 ppm) and non-flammable. Machine-room ventilation, leak detection, and operator certification requirements per ASHRAE Standard 15 are more stringent than for A-class refrigerants.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Low-pressure centrifugal chillers (large commercial buildings, district cooling, industrial process cooling)
    - Existing installed base (production banned 2020 for new equipment)
    - Servicing-only with reclaimed material

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-123 is under the Montreal Protocol HCFC phase-out, not the AIM Act (which targets HFCs). The US production phase-out completed January 1, 2020 — virgin R-123 is no longer manufactured. Reclaimed R-123 remains legal to use indefinitely under current regulations.

    For new low-pressure centrifugal chiller installations, the dominant replacement is R-1233zd(E) (Honeywell Solstice 1233zd, GWP 1) — a similar low-pressure HCFO that targets the same equipment envelope. R-514A and R-1224yd(Z) are also positioned in this niche but with smaller market share so far.

  serviceNotes: |
    Mineral oil (MO) is the standard lubricant for R-123. Alkylbenzene (AB) is also compatible. POE is NOT used with R-123 in original systems — that's an HFC requirement.

    R-123's B1 toxicity classification drives strict service procedures: machine-room ventilation per ASHRAE 15 (typically 1 CFM/ft² of machinery space plus emergency purge); refrigerant leak detection sensors with manned-room alarms; full-face respirator with appropriate cartridge for any service exposing the working fluid; EPA Section 608 Type II or Universal certification mandatory; specific manufacturer training for the equipment.

    Operating pressures are below atmospheric at typical evaporator temperatures — R-123 saturation at 70°F is -3 PSIG (vacuum). This is structural to the low-pressure chiller design and is part of why R-123 is classified as "low-pressure" — the entire low side typically operates in mild vacuum.

faqs:
  - q: "What does ASHRAE B1 classification mean for R-123?"
    a: "B1 is higher toxicity (Occupational Exposure Limit below 400 ppm) and non-flammable. The toxicity classification drives the machine-room safety requirements: ventilation per ASHRAE 15, refrigerant leak detection with manned-room alarms, respirator-rated PPE for service work, restricted occupancy classification for the chiller machinery space. R-123 is the only B-class refrigerant in widespread HVAC use; the requirements are well-codified but more stringent than for A1 refrigerants."

  - q: "Why does R-123 operate in vacuum?"
    a: "Low saturation pressures. R-123 saturation at typical chiller evaporator temperatures (35-45°F leaving water) is below atmospheric — typically 2-5 PSIG below ambient. This is structural to the low-pressure centrifugal chiller design and is part of why R-123 chillers are physically large relative to their capacity (the impeller and casing must be sized for the lower-density vapor). Air leaks INTO the system rather than refrigerant leaking OUT, requiring continuous purge units to evacuate non-condensables."

  - q: "Can I still buy R-123?"
    a: "Reclaimed R-123 only. Virgin US production ended January 1, 2020 under the Montreal Protocol HCFC phase-out. Reclaimed R-123 — recovered from end-of-life equipment, processed back to AHRI 700 specification, and resold — remains legal and available. Prices have risen substantially as the supply pool shrinks. Refrigerant management strategies for the remaining R-123 chiller installed base typically combine careful recovery during service, conversion-vs-replacement economic analysis, and consideration of HCFO replacement chillers (R-1233zd(E)) when equipment reaches end of life."

  - q: "What lubricant does R-123 use?"
    a: "Mineral oil (MO) is the standard. Alkylbenzene (AB) is also compatible. POE is NOT used with R-123 in original systems."

  - q: "What's replacing R-123 in new chillers?"
    a: "R-1233zd(E) (Honeywell Solstice 1233zd, also called Solstice LBA) is the dominant replacement — a HCFO with very low GWP (1), ASHRAE A1 (lower toxicity, non-flammable, easier machine-room requirements than R-123's B1), and similar pressure envelope. R-514A and R-1224yd(Z) target the same niche with smaller market share. New low-pressure centrifugal chiller installations are predominantly R-1233zd(E)."

  - q: "Is R-123 dangerous to work around?"
    a: "The OEL (50 ppm typical for R-123) means short-term exposure should be limited and respiratory protection is appropriate during service. The vapor is denser than air and accumulates at low points; chiller pits and mechanical-room sumps should be ventilated. Acute exposure causes central nervous system effects (dizziness, drowsiness); chronic exposure raises liver toxicity concerns. The risks are well-managed in standard chiller machine-room installations with proper ventilation and detection."

  - q: "What is the saturation pressure of R-123 at 70°F?"
    a: "Approximately -3 PSIG (vacuum). R-123's saturation pressure stays below atmospheric until around 75-80°F. This is structural to the low-pressure chiller design and is normal for R-123 systems — not an indication of leakage or low charge."
