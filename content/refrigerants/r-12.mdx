narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-12 is pure dichlorodifluoromethane — a chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) developed in the 1930s as a non-toxic, non-flammable replacement for the earlier ammonia and sulfur dioxide refrigerants. R-12 was the dominant mobile air conditioning refrigerant from the 1950s through the mid-1990s and the standard for domestic refrigerators for nearly as long.

    R-12 production in the US ended January 1, 1996 under the Montreal Protocol CFC phase-out. The chlorine in R-12 catalyzes ozone destruction in the stratosphere; the GWP of 10900 (IPCC AR5) makes it among the most climate-impactful common refrigerants per pound released.

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-12 production has been banned in the US since January 1, 1996 under the Montreal Protocol CFC phase-out (the most aggressive global environmental treaty). Reclaimed R-12 remains legal to use indefinitely for servicing pre-ban equipment, but the supply pool is essentially exhausted — most R-12 vehicles and refrigerators have been replaced through end-of-life cycles, and the reclaimed inventory has been progressively drawn down.

  serviceNotes: |
    Mineral oil (MO) is the lubricant standard. For the few remaining pre-1996 vehicles with R-12 AC, retrofit to R-134a or full system replacement are the only practical paths once a leak develops. EPA Section 608 certification is required to handle R-12; recovery is mandatory.

faqs:
  - q: "Why was R-12 banned?"
    a: "Stratospheric ozone depletion. R-12's chlorine content makes it ozone-depleting (ODP = 1.0 — R-12 is the reference for ODP). When R-12 reaches the stratosphere, UV light cleaves the C-Cl bond, releasing chlorine atoms that catalyze ozone destruction. The 1985 discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole accelerated international action; the 1987 Montreal Protocol mandated CFC phase-out and US production ended January 1, 1996. The protocol is the most successful international environmental treaty in history."

  - q: "Can I still get R-12?"
    a: "Reclaimed R-12 only, and supply is essentially exhausted. Pre-1996 inventory has been drawn down through service of remaining R-12 equipment. New US production is illegal. Reclamation from end-of-life vehicles and refrigerators continues at very low volumes. Pricing per pound is dramatically higher than modern refrigerants when material is available."

  - q: "What replaced R-12?"
    a: "Mobile AC: R-134a (1990s through 2014) and now R-1234yf. Domestic refrigeration: R-134a (most US, 1990s-2010s) and increasingly R-600a (isobutane). Commercial refrigeration: various HFC blends in the 1990s/2000s, now transitioning to lower-GWP A2L blends and natural refrigerants. The R-12 replacement trajectory in each application sets the template that's now repeating with the AIM Act HFC phase-down — refrigerants moving toward lower environmental impact through successive generations."

  - q: "What lubricant does R-12 use?"
    a: "Mineral oil (MO). Some alkylbenzene (AB) in later 1980s/1990s installations. R-12 mineral oil is not compatible with R-134a or any HFC refrigerant — retrofitting an R-12 system to R-134a requires oil change to PAG (mobile AC) or POE (stationary)."

  - q: "Is R-12 the same as Freon?"
    a: "'Freon' was DuPont's trademark for their refrigerant product line; Freon-12 specifically referred to R-12. Other manufacturers had their own brand names (Genetron from Honeywell/Allied, Forane from Arkema/Pennwalt). 'Freon' is often used colloquially to mean any refrigerant, but technically it was a brand. After patent expirations and the DuPont/Chemours split, the brand remains in Chemours' portfolio but is rarely used for current refrigerants."
