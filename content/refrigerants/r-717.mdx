narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-717 is pure ammonia (NH₃) — a natural refrigerant with zero ozone-depletion potential and zero global warming potential. It has been used in industrial refrigeration since the 1870s and remains the dominant refrigerant in food processing, cold storage warehouses, ice rinks, and petrochemical processing.

    R-717 is ASHRAE class B2L: higher toxicity (NIOSH IDLH 300 ppm) and mildly flammable (low burning velocity, ≤ 10 cm/s, with relatively narrow flammability limits in air of 15-28% by volume). This is the only B-class refrigerant in common HVAC use and the safety regime around it is correspondingly strict.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Industrial refrigeration — food processing, freezing tunnels, cold storage warehouses (the dominant application)
    - Ice rinks (both refrigerant and brine)
    - Petrochemical processing and gas separation
    - Large heat pumps (commercial and industrial)
    - District cooling and large chillers

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-717 is not subject to GWP-driven phase-down — it has zero GWP and zero ODP. The relevant regulations are around safety and accidental release:

    - EPA Risk Management Plan (RMP, 40 CFR 68) applies to facilities with > 10,000 lb ammonia charge
    - OSHA Process Safety Management (PSM, 29 CFR 1910.119) applies similarly
    - IIAR (International Institute of All-Natural Refrigeration) standards 2 (design), 6 (inspection), 7 (operations), 8 (decommissioning), and 9 (refrigerant management) define industry best practice
    - State and local fire codes typically reference IFC and IMC ammonia provisions

  serviceNotes: |
    Mineral oil (MO) is the historical standard. Alkylbenzene (AB) and polyalphaolefin (PAO) are also widely used. POE is NOT used with R-717.

    Ammonia attacks copper, brass, and many copper-bearing alloys. Industrial ammonia systems use steel piping, steel headers, and aluminum or stainless heat exchangers. Service personnel must be specifically trained for ammonia handling: SCBA (self-contained breathing apparatus), chemical-resistant gloves, eye protection, and emergency procedures.

    Ammonia's pungent odor is detectable at concentrations far below the IDLH (typically 5-50 ppm vs IDLH 300 ppm), providing a measure of natural warning before dangerous exposure. This is one of the reasons ammonia remains accepted in industrial settings despite its toxicity classification.

faqs:
  - q: "Is R-717 the same as household ammonia?"
    a: |
      Chemically yes, but refrigerant-grade ammonia is specified to 99.95% purity (per IIAR / ARI specifications). Household ammonia cleaner is typically 5-10% ammonia in water with surfactants and fragrances. Refrigerant ammonia is essentially water-free (water in ammonia systems causes corrosion and reduces capacity).

  - q: "Why is ammonia still used despite its toxicity?"
    a: |
      Multiple reasons. First, performance: ammonia has the highest refrigerating capacity per kilogram of any commonly-used refrigerant. Second, environmental: zero GWP, zero ODP, no phase-down risk. Third, cost: bulk ammonia is far cheaper than synthetic refrigerants. Fourth, safety record: the industrial-refrigeration regulatory regime (IIAR, RMP, PSM) has produced an excellent track record over a century of use.

      The toxicity is a real constraint — ammonia is rarely used outside industrial settings because the safety infrastructure (machine rooms, ventilation, leak detection, trained operators) isn't practical at smaller scales.

  - q: "What lubricant does ammonia use?"
    a: |
      Mineral oil is the historical standard. Polyalphaolefin (PAO) is increasingly used in modern systems for better viscosity stability across the wide temperature range typical of ammonia industrial applications. Alkylbenzene is also compatible. POE is NOT used with ammonia and must not be substituted.

  - q: "Why doesn't ammonia work in copper piping?"
    a: |
      Ammonia attacks copper, brass, and many copper-bearing alloys (such as bronze and silver-brazed joints) through both direct chemical reaction and stress-corrosion mechanisms. Industrial ammonia systems use carbon steel, stainless steel, and aluminum exclusively. Joints are welded rather than brazed.

  - q: "What is the operating pressure of an ammonia system?"
    a: |
      Comparable to R-22. At a 95°F condenser saturation, expect ~196 PSIG discharge. At a 20°F evaporator (typical for industrial cold storage), expect ~33 PSIG suction. Saturation pressures at intermediate temperatures: 50°F = 76 PSIG, 70°F = 114 PSIG, 100°F = 213 PSIG. See the chart above for the full range.

  - q: "What happens if there's an ammonia leak?"
    a: |
      The pungent odor (detectable at 5-50 ppm) typically warns occupants well before dangerous concentrations are reached. Evacuation is the first response. Ammonia is lighter than air and disperses readily outdoors; indoor leaks accumulate at ceiling level. Water sprays absorb ammonia vapor (ammonia is highly water-soluble). Trained ammonia response personnel use SCBA and chemical PPE. The IIAR Ammonia Refrigeration Foundation publishes the standard emergency response procedures.
