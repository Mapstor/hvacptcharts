narrative:
  whatItIs: |
    R-22 is pure chlorodifluoromethane (CHClF₂) — a single-molecule hydrochlorofluorocarbon, not a blend. From the 1960s through the early 2000s it was the dominant refrigerant in US residential central air conditioning, light commercial cooling, heat pumps, and medium-temperature commercial refrigeration.

    Its chlorine content gives it a non-zero ozone-depletion potential (ODP 0.055). Under the Montreal Protocol, R-22 production was phased down in stages: new equipment production banned in 2010, and virgin R-22 production banned entirely in the US on January 1, 2020. The service supply now comes from reclaimed and stockpiled material.

  whereItsUsed:
    - Existing residential central AC systems (servicing only — no new equipment since 2010)
    - Existing commercial chillers and heat pumps
    - Medium-temperature commercial refrigeration (legacy)
    - Service equipment for the millions of R-22 systems still in operation

  phaseDownStatus: |
    R-22 is subject to the Montreal Protocol HCFC phase-down, not the AIM Act (which targets HFCs). Key US milestones: 2010 ban on new equipment, 2020 ban on virgin production. Reclaimed R-22 remains legal to use indefinitely under current regulations.

    The practical reality: R-22 prices have climbed sharply as supply tightens. Repairs to leaking R-22 systems often cost more than partial system replacement. Most contractors advise full-system replacement (with R-410A or A2L equipment) when a major component fails on a legacy R-22 system.

  serviceNotes: |
    Mineral oil (MO) is the historical lubricant standard. Alkylbenzene (AB) oils are also compatible and were used in some 1990s-era systems. POE oil is NOT used with R-22 — POE is required only for HFC retrofits.

    EPA Section 608 certification is required to service R-22 equipment. Type II (high-pressure) or Universal certification covers R-22. Recovery is required; venting is illegal under Clean Air Act §608.

faqs:
  - q: "Is R-22 a blend?"
    a: |
      No. R-22 is pure chlorodifluoromethane (CHClF₂), a single-molecule HCFC. This is sometimes confused because newer refrigerants are blends, but R-22 itself has no temperature glide and no composition fractions — it is a pure substance.

  - q: "Can I still buy R-22?"
    a: |
      Reclaimed R-22 is legal to buy and use. Virgin production was banned in the US on January 1, 2020. The supply comes from material recovered during equipment removal, processed back to specification, and resold. Prices have risen significantly as the supply tightens.

  - q: "What lubricant does R-22 use?"
    a: |
      Mineral oil (MO) is the standard. Alkylbenzene (AB) is also compatible. Polyolester (POE) oil — required for HFC refrigerants — is NOT used with R-22 in original systems. Adding POE to a working R-22 system serves no purpose and creates contamination risk.

  - q: "What is the normal operating pressure of R-22?"
    a: |
      At 95°F outdoor ambient on a properly charged residential R-22 system, expect roughly 70 PSIG suction and 250 PSIG discharge. Saturation pressure at 95°F is 181 PSIG; actual operating pressures depend on superheat, subcooling, ambient, and indoor load. See [/what-pressure-should-r22-be/](/what-pressure-should-r22-be/) for the diagnostic ranges.

  - q: "Can I retrofit R-22 to R-410A?"
    a: |
      Not as a drop-in. R-410A operates at substantially higher pressures than R-22 and requires POE oil rather than mineral oil. A true R-410A conversion requires replacing the compressor, expansion device, and possibly the indoor coil — usually more expensive than a full system replacement.

      For a true mid-life retrofit, the R-22 drop-in blends are R-407C, R-422D, R-438A (Honeywell Genetron MO99), and R-427A (Arkema Forane 427A). Each has tradeoffs in capacity, oil compatibility, and glide.

  - q: "Why is R-22 being phased out?"
    a: |
      R-22 contains chlorine, which depletes the stratospheric ozone layer when released. The Montreal Protocol (1987) mandated the phase-down of all ozone-depleting substances. R-22, as the most common HCFC, was a primary target. Replacement HFCs like R-410A have zero ODP — but their high GWP has made them in turn the target of the AIM Act phase-down.
